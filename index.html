About

I study how households form beliefs about the economy — particularly about inflation — and how the information they hold, and the frictions that shape it, feed back into their financial decisions. My dissertation brings together three papers at the intersection of household finance, expectation formation, and information frictions.

Before returning to academia I spent several years as a macroeconomist across leading policy institutions. I worked in the European Department of the International Monetary Fund, in the Prospects Group of the World Bank, in DG-Economics at the European Central Bank, and in the Regulatory Policy Division of the OECD. This work grounds my research in the questions that policymakers actually face.

I hold an MSc in the Political Economy of Europe from the London School of Economics and a BSc in Economics from University College London.
